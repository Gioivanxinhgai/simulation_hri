AbstractŌĆöHuman motion prediction plays a critical role in
ensuring safe and efficient coordination in humanŌĆōrobot collabo-
ration within manufacturing and service environments. However,
human movements exhibit nonlinear and stochastic characteristics,
making accurate prediction and reliability assessment challenging.
To tackle these issues, the present study introduces a model that
combines Gaussian process regression (GPR) with Elbow method-
based K-Means, allowing both motion prediction and uncertainty
quantification through predictive variance while significantly
reducing computational complexity. Results from two-dimensional
motion prediction demonstrate that the proposed model achieves
reliable prediction accuracy compared with standard GPR while
significantly reducing the computational load, thereby decreasing
inference time by approximately a factor of eight. Furthermore,
the model effectively captures predictive uncertainty, reflecting
performance degradation during abrupt changes in human motion.

prevalent in modern manufacturing and service industries,

accomplish shared tasks [1]ŌĆō[3]. In such collaborative settings,
humans contribute cognitive strengths, decision-making, and
the flexibility to react to unexpected problems in unstructured
environments, whereas robots offer high precision, compu-
tational capability, and consistency. The primary objective
of HRC systems is to achieve seamless cooperation while
ensuring safety, ergonomics, and efficiency [4], [5]. A key
challenge in achieving this objective is that robots must
proactively anticipate human intentions, rather than merely

human motion is inherently nonlinear and stochastic, relying
solely on predictions without uncertainty estimates may produce
overconfident robot behaviors that are unsafe when unexpected
motion changes occur. Therefore, assessing the uncertainty of
the predictions enables the robot to make informed decisions
and adopt conservative behaviors when confidence is low.
Driven by this challenge, the prediction of human motion
has received significant attention in the robotics research
community [7]ŌĆō[9], with existing approaches broadly cat-
egorized into model-based and data-driven methodologies.
Model-based methods formulate explicit mathematical or
physical models to describe and predict human behaviors.
For example, the approach in [10] employed an Unscented
Kalman filter integrated with an interacting multiple model
estimator to predict future human poses based on purely
kinematic double integrator models. Taking a different model-
based approach, [11] combined a biomechanical model and
inverse reinforcement learning to generate explainable motion
predictions, integrating them into a model predictive control
scheme for safe handovers. However, human movements are
inherently nonlinear, stochastic, and highly individual-specific
in the event of significant trajectory perturbations or sudden
changes in motion patterns. To overcome this limitation,
data-driven methods have become increasingly prevalent in
recent years [12]ŌĆō[14]. These approaches learn motion patterns
directly from observed data through statistical modeling and
machine learning techniques. One of the most widely adopted
architectures is recurrent neural networks (RNNs), owing to
their ability to capture temporal dependencies in sequential
motion data. Yet, standard RNNs suffer from the vanishing
gradient problem, limiting their ability to capture long-term
dependencies. Advanced variants such as Long Short-Term
Memory (LSTM) networks and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs)
overcome this limitation, enabling more effective modeling
of the nonlinear and stochastic dynamics of human motion.
For instance, [13] used a deep LSTM to predict motion in
co-carrying tasks, yet prediction errors in practice remain
unavoidable due to noise and motion variability. Building
on RNN foundations, [14] proposed a residual sequence-to-
sequence architecture with a single GRU, effectively resolving
first-frame discontinuities while reducing computational cost
compared to multi-layer LSTM approaches. Despite these
advances, these techniques typically require large training
datasets and lack uncertainty quantification mechanisms, which
increases risks in pHRI tasks. To mitigate these limitations,
Gaussian process regression (GPR) has been proposed to offer
a non-parametric Bayesian framework that not only learns
effectively from moderate-sized datasets [8], but also provides
uncertainty estimates via its predictive variance [15]ŌĆō[18]. In
pHRI, reliable uncertainty quantification is crucial for enabling
the robotic system to make informed decisions and adopt
conservative behaviors when prediction confidence is low,
thereby preventing collisions. However, standard GPR suffers
from O(N 3) time complexity due to the required inversion
of the N ├Ś N covariance matrix, where N represents the
number of training samples [19]. This computational burden
becomes infeasible for real-time applications as the training
data expands.
Motivated by the aforementioned analysis, this paper pro-
poses a K-Means-based GPR model (KMGPR), in which the
Elbow method-based K-Means serves as a Subset of Data
(SoD) strategy to select representative samples, significantly
reducing the computational burden in HRC applications. The
main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
First, KMGPR achieves prediction accuracy nearly equivalent to
standard GPR while reducing inference time by approximately
eight times using only 20% of the original training samples.
Second, two representative HRC scenarios are considered to
demonstrate the KMGPRŌĆÖs uncertainty quantification capabil-
ity and prediction performance compared to standard GPR.
Furthermore, a theoretical analysis is provided to explain the
behavioral differences between KMGPR and standard GPR
under abrupt motion changes.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. The
problem of human motion prediction is formulated as a
regression task in Section II. Section III presents the GPR
framework integrated with the Elbow method-based K-Means
for computational efficiency. Section IV provides validation
setup and performance evaluation across two specific scenarios.
Finally, the conclusions and future works are presented in
Section V.

C. Elbow method-based K-Means
In this study, the Elbow method-based K-Means is em-
ployed to select a representative SoD from the full training
dataset. Given a predefined number of clusters K, the algo-
rithm partitions the input set {X1, . . . , XN} into K clusters
{S1, . . . , SK} through the following iterative steps:
1) Initialization: Select K data points randomly from the
training set to serve as the initial centroids.
2) Cluster Assignment: For each data point Xi, calculate
the Euclidean distance to each centroid and assign it to
the nearest cluster.
3) Centroid Update: Compute the mean of all data points
assigned to each cluster Sk to update its centroid Ck.
4) Iteration: Repeat steps (2) and (3) until the cluster
assignments no longer change.

navigated towards three distinct targets within 10 seconds at a
sampling interval of 0.1 s. According to the validation split in
Table I, 24 trajectories were allocated for the training phase,
while the remaining 6 were used to validate the training process.

B. Validation Results and Analytics
1) Scenario 1 - Nominal Trajectories: In this scenario, both
models including KMGPR and GPR are evaluated on nominal
trajectories toward three learned targets. The quantitative results
in Table II show that KMGPR achieves an overall MSE of
0.21├Ś10ŌłÆ6 m2, while standard GPR achieves 0.19├Ś10ŌłÆ6 m2,
demonstrating that both models maintain nearly equivalent
prediction accuracy across all three targets. Regarding com-
putational efficiency, KMGPR reduces the prediction time by
approximately a factor of eight compared to standard GPR.
This is attributed to the elbow method-based K-Means, which
extracts a SoD of M = 484 samples from the initial training
set of N = 2424, which is then utilized in the prediction phase
as illustrated in Fig. 3.
The uncertainty estimation capability of the KMGPR model
is evaluated using the 95% confidence interval. As depicted in
Fig. 6, despite relying on merely 20% of the original data, the
KMGPR model maintains consistently low uncertainty across
three predefined targets, demonstrating that the extracted SoD
still provides sufficient data to yield high-confidence predictions
in nominal scenarios.
The results of Scenario 1 confirm that applying K-Means to
select a representative subset still maintains prediction accuracy
nearly equivalent to standard GPR, while preserving uncertainty
estimation capability and reducing significant computational
workload.
2) Scenario 2 - Abrupt Motion Changes: In Scenario 2,
both models are evaluated on an atypical trajectory with abrupt
changes in motion. Specifically, the trajectory consists of two
distinct phases: the human operator first accelerates from t =
3.3 s, then stops abruptly at t = 4.0 s. When evaluated over
the entire trajectory, standard GPR achieves a slightly lower
overall MSE than KMGPR (4.42├Ś10ŌłÆ6 m2 versus 5.23├Ś10ŌłÆ6
m2). However, this metric does not fully capture the behavioral
differences observed across distinct phases of this scenario.
As the human operator begins accelerating around t = 3.3 s,
the predictions exhibit inherent latency, failing to track the
motion instantaneously and thus falling behind the ground
truth. Subsequently, as the motion decelerates and stops
abruptly at t = 4.0 s, both models overshoot the stationary
position. Notably, the uncertainty estimates of both models
expand significantly at this stage, indicating a sharp drop in
prediction confidence because such sudden deceleration patterns
are absent from the training data. From this point, the two
models exhibit markedly different behaviors. As shown in
Fig.7(c), after overshooting, KMGPR returns to the stationary
position along a stable recovery trajectory without chattering.
In contrast, Fig.7(f) shows that standard GPR exhibits a
highly oscillatory trajectory, making a wider deviation before
eventually converging to the stationary position. This difference

V. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
This paper proposed a KMGPR model for the human motion
prediction problem in HRC. By utilizing K-Means clustering to
select a representative subset, the proposed method significantly
reduces computational complexity compared to standard GPR,
while maintaining motion prediction accuracy and uncertainty
quantification through predictive variance. Validation results
demonstrate that the model maintains prediction accuracy nearly
equivalent to standard GPR while reducing computation time
by approximately eight times. In the abrupt motion change
scenario, KMGPR exhibits greater prediction stability with
significantly fewer oscillations compared to standard GPR.
Future work will focus on validating with real-world pHRI in
three-dimensional framework and developing adaptive schemes
based on predicted uncertainty.
